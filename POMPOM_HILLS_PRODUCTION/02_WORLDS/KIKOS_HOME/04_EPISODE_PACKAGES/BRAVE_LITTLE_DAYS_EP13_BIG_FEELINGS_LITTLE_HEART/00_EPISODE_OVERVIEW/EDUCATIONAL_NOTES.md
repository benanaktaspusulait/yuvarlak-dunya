# Educational Notes — EP13

## Learning Objectives

### Primary Objective
Children will understand that **sadness is a normal emotion** and learn that **naming feelings** and **breathing** can help them feel better.

### Secondary Objectives
1. Children will identify the physical sensations of sadness
2. Children will learn a simple breathing technique
3. Children will understand that feelings pass with time and support
4. Children will see peer support modeled positively

## Key Concepts Taught

### 1. Naming Emotions
**What children learn:** When you feel something, you can say what it is.

**In the episode:**
- Kiko says "I feel sad"
- This simple act begins her recovery

**Real-world application:**
- Children can practice naming their own feelings
- "I feel mad." "I feel scared." "I feel happy."

### 2. Validation
**What children learn:** All feelings are okay to have.

**In the episode:**
- Mimi says "It's okay to feel sad"
- She doesn't try to fix it or dismiss it

**Real-world application:**
- Adults can validate children's feelings
- "It's okay to feel scared. I understand."

### 3. Breathing as a Tool
**What children learn:** Taking deep breaths helps when you feel big feelings.

**In the episode:**
- Mimi guides Kiko: "In... and out..."
- Kiko feels better afterward

**Real-world application:**
- Children can practice this technique
- "Belly breathing" or "balloon breathing"

### 4. Feelings Pass
**What children learn:** Sadness doesn't last forever.

**In the episode:**
- Kiko is sad in Shot 02
- By Shot 06, she feels better
- Quick, realistic resolution

**Real-world application:**
- Children learn patience with their emotions
- "This feeling will pass."

## Research Basis

### Emotional Regulation in Young Children
- Children ages 2-6 are developing emotional regulation skills
- Naming emotions helps activate the prefrontal cortex (brain cooling)
- Deep breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system
- Peer support provides co-regulation

### Key Studies
- **Gottman (1997):** Emotion coaching improves children's regulation
- **Siegel (2012):** "Name it to tame it" — labeling emotions reduces intensity
- **Zinsser (2016):** Social-emotional learning improves academic outcomes
