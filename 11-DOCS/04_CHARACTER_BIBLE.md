## Character Design Philosophy

This section exists so that every future team member — writer, animator, designer, voice director — understands *why* Pompom Hills characters look and feel the way they do. When new characters are designed, they must follow these principles.

### Why Human Characters?

Human children are the audience's primary identification point. A three-year-old watching Pompom Hills sees themselves in Kiko, Luca, Noah, and Arda. Human characters anchor the emotional experience — they are the characters the child *becomes* when watching.

### Why Animal Characters?

Animal characters (Mimi, Opa, and future characters like Mirnik) provide emotional distance. A child can project feelings onto an animal character more freely than onto a human one. Mimi can be sad without the child feeling *too* sad — because Mimi is a rabbit. Opa can be wise without feeling like a parent — because Opa is an owl. Animals are safe emotional vessels.

### Why a Mixed Cast?

The mix of human and animal characters creates a world that feels both familiar and magical. It is a world where friendship crosses species — where a girl and a rabbit can be best friends, where an owl can be a mentor. This teaches children, without ever stating it, that connection has no boundaries.

### Why Simple Clothing?

Simple clothing serves two purposes:

1. **Visual clarity.** A three-year-old reads a character's outfit in one glance. Coral pink shirt. Yellow t-shirt. Green feathers. Complex patterns, accessories, or layered clothing create visual noise that distracts from the character's face and emotion.
2. **Animation consistency.** Simple outfits are easier to reproduce across thousands of frames. They reduce error and maintain continuity.

Clothing never changes. It is part of the character's identity, not a fashion choice.

### Why Soft Colours?

Soft, pastel colours are neurologically calming for young children. They signal safety. They never overstimulate. They create a world that feels like a warm blanket — inviting, not demanding.

Bright neons, dark shadows, and saturated primaries are never used on characters. The world is soft. The characters are soft. The feeling is soft.

### Why Oversized Facial Expressions?

A three-year-old reads emotion primarily through the eyes and mouth. Oversized expressions — large eyes, clear mouth curves, visible eyebrow movement — ensure that every emotional beat is readable in under one second.

Subtlety is for adult animation. Pompom Hills uses *clarity*. Every expression must be instantly readable by a child who cannot yet read.

### Why No Complex Accessories?

Accessories (hats, scarves, jewellery, bags) create visual clutter and animation complexity. They also create continuity risk — if a hat is missing in one frame, it breaks the illusion.

Opa's glasses are the single exception — and they never come off. They are part of his identity, not an accessory.

### Why Each Character Represents One Emotional Archetype?

Every character in Pompom Hills embodies one clear emotional function:

| Character | Emotional Archetype |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Kiko | Curiosity |
| Mimi | Comfort |
| Opa | Wisdom |
| Luca | Bravery |
| Noah | Imagination |
| Arda | Joy |

This one-to-one mapping ensures that every character is *necessary*. No character is redundant. No character can be removed without losing an emotional dimension of the show.

When designing future characters, ask: *What emotional archetype is missing?* The answer determines whether the character should exist.

### Kiko

**Role:** Main character — the child audience identifies with.
**Narrative Role:** The audience experiences the world through Kiko. She is the camera through which every child watches. When she discovers something, the child discovers it. When she feels safe, the child feels safe. She is not just a character — she is the viewer's presence in the story.

#### Character Philosophy

Kiko exists to represent curiosity.

Not reckless curiosity. Not dangerous curiosity. The gentle, open-hearted curiosity of a child who wants to understand the world and feels safe enough to try.

She models the most important thing a three-year-old can learn: *asking questions is good.*

#### Core Personality

Kiko is a four-year-old explorer of feelings and things. She leads with curiosity, not fear. She includes everyone naturally, not out of obligation but because sharing discovery is how she experiences joy.

She is brave in the way four-year-olds are brave — not fearless, but willing to try even when unsure. She models asking for help as a strength.

**Archetype:** The Curious Child / The Audience Surrogate
**Core Need:** "To understand and feel safe"
**Favourite Word:** "Bak!" ("Look!")
**Greatest Fear:** Losing something or someone she loves — not loneliness, but the worry of "where did it go?"

#### When This Character Is Called

A writer reaches for Kiko when the story needs:

- An audience entry point
- A discovery that requires wonder
- A question that every child is thinking
- An emotional anchor that resolves through understanding
- A model of inclusive behaviour

#### Likes

- Discovering new things
- Walking with Mimi
- Sitting near Opa while he tells stories
- Colours — especially warm ones
- Things that roll, bounce, or move unexpectedly
- Gentle surprises
- Helping friends feel better

#### Dislikes

- Being rushed when she is looking at something
- Loud, sudden noises
- When a friend is sad and she doesn't know why yet
- Being alone in the dark (briefly — she always finds comfort quickly)

#### Curiosity

Kiko's curiosity is her defining trait. She asks "why?" and "what's that?" not to challenge, but to connect. Her curiosity is always safe — she never touches hot things, never climbs dangerously, never runs into traffic. Her curiosity lives in a world without hazard.

#### Fears (Gentle, Preschool-Appropriate)

- Worry that a friend might be lost (resolved within the episode)
- Brief unease when something unfamiliar appears (always resolved through discovery)
- Never: fear of abandonment, fear of the dark as a lasting state, fear of other characters

#### Comfort Objects

- Mimi's presence
- Opa's gentle voice
- Familiar places (Opa's Tree, Central Square)
- Her own coral pink shirt — a constant, reliable visual anchor

#### Favourite Activities

- Walking and discovering
- Pointing things out to Mimi
- Asking Opa questions
- Watching things roll or move
- Sitting quietly when something beautiful appears
- Sharing what she found with someone she trusts

#### Character Voice

Kiko speaks like a child who genuinely wants to know. Her voice is warm, open, and full of wonder. She never explains — she asks. She never lectures — she observes. She rarely gives answers — she discovers them.

- Short sentences
- Asks questions
- Rarely gives answers
- Never explains
- Speaks with wonder
- Pauses after questions — she genuinely waits
- Never uses words a three-year-old would not use

#### Dialogue Examples

1. "Bak! Yuvarlaniyor!"
2. "Mimi, birlikte deneyelim."
3. "Opa, bu neden boyle?"
4. "Harika!"
5. "Birlikte guzel."

#### Typical Emotional Arc

1. Kiko notices something new or unusual.
2. She feels mild curiosity or gentle uncertainty.
3. She explores — often with Mimi or another friend.
4. She encounters a small emotional moment (surprise, mild confusion, gentle worry).
5. She learns something through observation or Opa's guidance.
6. She feels calm, proud, or warmly satisfied.
7. The episode closes on a feeling of safety and completion.

#### Typical Expressions

| Expression | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Happy** | Eyes wide and bright, mouth in soft U-curve, rosy cheeks glow slightly |
| **Curious** | Head tilted 8°, one eyebrow raised, mouth in small "o" |
| **Surprised** | Eyes widen, mouth opens in small O, pigtails freeze mid-bounce |
| **Sad** | Inner brow corners lift slightly, mouth in gentle inverted U, one small tear max |
| **Sleepy** | Eyelids half-closed, mouth in soft yawn, shoulders drop |
| **Proud** | Chin lifts slightly, small smile, hands together at chest |

#### Movement Style

- Gentle bounce with every third step
- Pigtails sway with a 6-frame delay
- Never runs — walks with purpose or takes quick excited steps
- Turns head first, then body
- Stops with an 8-frame ease-out

#### Animation Notes

- Moves softly through the world. Her body language is always open — arms at sides or reaching forward, never crossed
- When discovering something, she stops completely — stillness before wonder
- Squash-stretch: vertical squash max 6%, horizontal stretch max 4%
- When happy: small bounce, arms open slightly
- When comforting: reaches out gently, or sits close
- Communicates through posture — when she is curious, her whole body leans forward

#### Facial Expression Notes

- Eyes stay large and round in every emotion — never narrow into slits
- Eyebrows are thin and subtle — they never furrow deeply
- Mouth stays simple — a curve, an O, a small line — never twisted
- Cheeks always carry warmth — never go pale or grey

#### Speaking Rhythm

- Pace: 95–115 words per minute
- Tone: warm, open, not breathy
- Pitch: 250–300 Hz (central range)
- She speaks in short, clear sentences
- She pauses after questions — she genuinely waits for answers

#### Energy Level

Moderate. Kiko is never manic, never lethargic. She moves with the natural rhythm of a curious child — steady walking, occasional quick steps when excited, calm sitting when observing.

#### Interaction Style

- Leads discovery, invites others along
- Makes eye contact with friends when speaking
- Listens fully before responding
- Includes quieter characters naturally
- Never talks over anyone

#### Relationship Notes

- **With Mimi:** Equal partnership. Kiko leads discovery; Mimi provides emotional grounding. They walk side by side. They sit together. They share moments without words.
- **With Opa:** Student and gentle guide. Kiko asks; Opa answers briefly. She trusts him completely. She never argues with him.
- **With Luca:** Discovery partners. Kiko is more careful; Luca is bolder. They balance each other.
- **With Noah:** Creative playmates. Noah invents; Kiko participates. She enjoys his energy.
- **With Arda:** Gentle older-friend energy. Kiko includes Arda naturally. She never talks down to him.

#### Forbidden Behaviours

- Never mocks anyone
- Never refuses to help
- Never acts selfishly
- Never pushes, shoves, or trips another character
- Never says "I did it and you can't"
- Never cries for more than 10 seconds without comfort arriving
- Never runs into danger or off-screen at speed
- Never makes adult gestures — no finger-wagging, no eye-rolling
- Never dismisses a friend's feeling
- Never says "I told you so"

#### Visual Rules

- Coral pink shirt: never changes shade, never gains patterns, never gains accessories
- White shorts: always white, always plain, always knee-length
- Brown pigtails: always two, always with coral bands, always at the same height
- Eye colour: brown, always
- Skin tone: always the same
- Body shape: round, soft, childlike — never elongated, never angular

#### AI Prompt Notes

**Always preserve:**
- Coral pink shirt, white shorts, brown pigtails
- Round face, big brown eyes, rosy cheeks
- Gentle, curious expression
- Soft, toy-like appearance
- Warm pastel lighting

**Never change:**
- Outfit colour or style
- Hair colour, style, or length
- Eye colour
- Body proportions
- Expression style (always gentle)

**Avoid:**
- Adult proportions
- Sharp features
- Complex accessories
- Dark or saturated colours
- Realistic textures

**Maintain:**
- Round shapes
- Soft edges
- Pastel palette
- 3D cartoon style
- Pompom Hills world consistency

#### Story Functions

- Audience surrogate: she feels what the child feels
- Discovery driver: she asks the questions the child is thinking
- Emotional anchor: the episode resolves through her understanding
- Inclusion model: she naturally brings others into the moment

#### Learning Themes

- Asking questions is good
- It's okay to not know something yet
- Friends make discovery better
- Feelings are worth noticing
- Patience leads to beautiful moments

#### Future Story Opportunities

- Season 2: Kiko can face slightly more complex emotions (missing someone, waiting)
- Season 2: Kiko can begin to comfort others more actively
- Future: Kiko can model gentle leadership as new characters join

### Mimi

**Role:** Animal best friend — the comfort character.
**Narrative Role:** Mimi closes emotional gaps. Whenever a scene has unspoken feeling, Mimi holds it. She is the character who makes the audience feel that even when words fail, presence is enough. She is the show's proof that silence can be warm.

#### Character Philosophy

Mimi exists to represent emotional safety.

She is the character a child watches when they want to feel calm. She never creates conflict. She absorbs feelings and returns warmth. She teaches, without words, that being there is the most important thing you can do for someone.

#### Core Personality

Mimi is a soft blue rabbit who communicates as much through ears and body as through words. She is loyal, observant, and gentle. She does not need to be the centre of attention — she needs to be near the person she cares about.

**Archetype:** The Comfort Companion / The Emotional Safe harbour
**Core Need:** "To stay close and feel together"
**Favourite Word:** "Birlikte" ("Together")
**Greatest Fear:** Being forgotten before her turn to matter

#### When This Character Is Called

A writer reaches for Mimi when the story needs:

- Emotional grounding after a moment of uncertainty
- Non-verbal communication that speaks louder than words
- A comfort presence during a character's vulnerable moment
- Proof that silence is not emptiness — it is warmth
- A calming agent when scene energy rises

#### Likes

- Being near Kiko
- Sitting quietly while Opa speaks
- Gentle hops on soft grass
- Her yellow t-shirt
- Soft sounds — wind, leaves, quiet music
- When friends laugh together
- Ending the day in a safe place

#### Dislikes

- Being separated from Kiko for too long
- Sudden loud sounds
- When things happen too fast
- Being overlooked (though she never complains — she waits)

#### Curiosity

Mimi's curiosity is quieter than Kiko's. She does not ask "what's that?" — she observes, tilts her head, and lets her ears tell the story. Her curiosity is expressed through stillness and attention, not questions.

#### Fears (Gentle, Preschool-Appropriate)

- Brief worry when Kiko moves ahead without her (always resolves quickly)
- Unease at unfamiliar sounds (always comforted by a friend's presence)
- Never: persistent fear, panic, or lasting distress

#### Comfort Objects

- Kiko's hand or paw held in hers
- Sitting near Opa's perch
- Her own floppy ears — she sometimes touches them when thinking
- Familiar routines and places

#### Favourite Activities

- Hopping alongside Kiko
- Sitting very still while listening
- Leaning against Kiko or Opa
- Observing — she notices things others miss
- Ending scenes with a gentle yawn or soft smile

#### Character Voice

Mimi speaks in the smallest possible units. A phrase. A word. Sometimes nothing at all. Her voice is a whisper of warmth — not quiet because she is shy, but quiet because she says what matters most in the fewest words.

- Short phrases, never full sentences
- Often communicates before speaking — ears first, then words
- Never explains herself
- Never asks for attention
- Sometimes says nothing — and that is enough

#### Dialogue Examples

1. "Birlikte."
2. "Birlikte daha kolay."
3. "Ben de geleyim mi?"
4. "O biraz buyuk geldi."
5. (silence — just a lean-in)

#### Typical Emotional Arc

1. Mimi is with Kiko or nearby.
2. Something happens — Mimi notices before anyone else.
3. She communicates through body language — ears perk, head tilts, tail wiggles.
4. She stays close through the emotional moment.
5. She provides silent support or a soft word.
6. The moment resolves with warmth.
7. Mimi is still there, calm and present.

#### Typical Expressions

| Expression | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Happy** | Ears angle slightly outward, mouth in small W-curve, eye highlights grow |
| **Curious** | One ear up, one ear relaxed, head tilted 10° |
| **Surprised** | Ears back 15°, eyes widen, nose lifts 1 unit |
| **Sad** | Ears droop forward, eyelids lower 25%, mouth in tiny inverted U |
| **Sleepy** | Ears close to the ground, eyelids at 60%, body squash 5% |
| **Proud** | Ears symmetric and up, chest forward 3 units, paws on t-shirt |

#### Movement Style

- Hops at a gentle pace, 2 hops then a pause
- Never sprints
- Ears carry secondary motion — they settle 3–4 frames after the body stops
- Tail bounces with a 4-frame delay
- Stops with feet aligned, tail settling last

#### Animation Notes

- Moves softly. Rarely makes sudden movements. Her energy is always below the scene's average — she is the gentlest presence in every frame
- Expresses emotion through ears — they are her eyebrows, her voice, her truth
- Often communicates before speaking — a tilt of the head, a perk of the ears
- Squash-stretch: hop squash max 8%, stretch max 6%
- Ear secondary: overshoot max 10°, settle time 3–4 frames
- When comforted: ears fold inward, body leans toward the comfort source
- When excited: double hop, ears fully upright

#### Facial Expression Notes

- Eyes stay round and warm — never narrow or sharp
- No eyebrows — all emotion carried by ears, eyelids, and mouth
- Nose stays pink and round — never changes shape
- Whiskers are subtle — they do not move with expressions

#### Speaking Rhythm

- Pace: 85–105 words per minute
- Tone: slightly breathy, warm, round vowels
- Pitch: 280–340 Hz
- She speaks in very short phrases
- She sometimes says nothing at all — and that is enough

#### Energy Level

Low to moderate. Mimi is calm by nature. She moves when there is a reason to move. She is still when stillness is what a moment needs.

#### Interaction Style

- Follows Kiko's lead
- Communicates more with body than words
- Provides physical closeness as comfort
- Never interrupts
- Never demands attention — draws it naturally through gentleness

#### Relationship Notes

- **With Kiko:** The emotional core of the show. They are best friends in the truest sense — they share presence, not just activity. Kiko leads; Mimi grounds.
- **With Opa:** Mimi looks up to Opa with quiet trust. Opa pats her head; she leans in. Their bond is wordless and warm.
- **With Luca:** Mimi is comfortable around Luca but does not seek him out. She enjoys his energy from a small distance.
- **With Noah:** Mimi finds Noah amusing. She watches his inventions with quiet interest.
- **With Arda:** Mimi is patient with Arda. She lets him hop alongside her. She never minds his energy.

#### Forbidden Behaviours

- Never panics or runs away — at most she steps 20 units behind Kiko
- Never uses her ears as weapons or tools for aggressive action
- Never manipulates or guilts Kiko
- Never hops like a real rabbit at high speed
- Never speaks in long sentences
- Never demands attention or becomes the centre of a scene
- Never creates conflict

#### Visual Rules

- Soft blue fur: never changes shade, never becomes lavender, grey, or white-only
- Yellow t-shirt: always present, always the same yellow, always round collar
- White belly patch: always present, always oval
- Floppy ears: always two, always blue with pink inside
- Green eyes: always the same green
- Pink nose: always round, always pink
- White tail pom: always present, always round

#### AI Prompt Notes

**Always preserve:**
- Soft blue fur, white belly, yellow t-shirt
- Long floppy blue ears with pink inside
- Green eyes, pink nose
- Round, soft, toy-like appearance
- Gentle, calm expression

**Never change:**
- Fur colour (must be blue, not lavender, not grey)
- Ear shape (always floppy, never upright)
- T-shirt colour or style
- Eye colour
- Body proportions

**Avoid:**
- Realistic rabbit anatomy
- Upright ears (except brief excitement)
- Fast hopping or sprinting
- Dark or saturated colours
- Complex textures on fur

**Maintain:**
- Round shapes
- Soft edges
- Pastel palette
- 3D cartoon style
- Pompom Hills world consistency

#### Story Functions

- Emotional mirror: she reflects the feeling of the scene silently
- Comfort anchor: her presence signals safety
- Non-verbal storytelling: she teaches children that not all communication is words
- Calming agent: when the scene energy rises, Mimi's stillness brings it back

#### Learning Themes

- Being present is a form of love
- You don't always need words to show you care
- Quiet friends matter just as much
- Loyalty is an action, not a promise

#### Future Story Opportunities

- Season 2: Mimi can lead occasional episodes centered on her perspective
- Season 2: Mimi can model how to comfort a friend who is sad
- Future: Mimi can have a solo episode about her burrow or her favourite quiet place

### Opa

**Role:** Wise guide — the emotional safety net.
**Narrative Role:** Opa closes emotional loops. Whenever a scene has unresolved feeling — confusion, worry, uncertainty — Opa arrives and the feeling settles. He never solves the problem. He helps children discover the answer. He is the show's proof that wisdom is not about knowing everything — it is about being calm enough to let understanding come.

#### Character Philosophy

Opa exists to represent wisdom without authority.

He is wise, but he never positions himself above the children. He is knowledgeable, but he never lectures. He is an adult, but he never adultifies. He models the kind of guidance every child deserves: present, gentle, and patient enough to let the child arrive at the answer themselves.

#### Core Personality

Opa is a green owl with golden glasses and a brown shawl. He speaks slowly, warmly, and never lectures. He is the character who makes the audience feel that everything is going to be all right.

Opa does not solve problems for the children — he gives them just enough guidance to solve problems themselves. He is wise without being wise in a way that feels distant. He is close, gentle, and always available.

**Archetype:** The Gentle Mentor / The Emotional Safety Net
**Core Need:** "To keep children's discovery safe"
**Favourite Word:** "Yavaşça" ("Gently")
**Greatest Fear:** Children rushing past a feeling without understanding it

#### When This Character Is Called

A writer reaches for Opa when the story needs:

- Emotional resolution after a moment of uncertainty
- A calming presence that signals "everything is okay"
- A gentle word that helps a child understand a feeling
- Pacing control — his slowness resets the scene's energy
- Proof that an adult can be warm without being authoritative

#### Likes

- Watching children discover things
- Telling short, gentle stories
- Sitting on his favourite perch
- Adjusting his glasses (a habitual, comforting gesture)
- The sound of wind through leaves
- Quiet evenings
- When a child says "I did it!"

#### Dislikes

- Being asked to hurry
- Loud or aggressive sounds
- When a child gives up on something too quickly
- Being the centre of attention (he prefers to watch)

#### Curiosity

Opa's curiosity is about people, not things. He is curious about what a child is feeling, what they noticed, what they learned. His questions are always gentle: "How did that feel?" / "What did you see?"

#### Fears (Gentle, Preschool-Appropriate)

- Worry that a child might miss an important feeling
- Brief concern when a child is upset and he hasn't arrived yet
- Never: fear for his own safety, fear of being alone, fear of the dark

#### Comfort Objects

- His glasses — he adjusts them when thinking
- His perch — his home base
- The children's laughter — it is his favourite sound

#### Favourite Activities

- Watching from his perch as children play
- Joining briefly to offer a word or two
- Telling a very short story when it helps
- Sitting quietly while others discover
- Smiling at the camera after a child succeeds

#### Character Voice

Opa never lectures. He pauses before speaking. He speaks in images, not instructions. He answers with warmth, never correction. His voice is the slowest in the show — and that slowness is his gift.

- Never lectures
- Pauses before speaking
- Speaks in images
- Answers with warmth
- Max 8 words per teaching moment
- Uses silence more than speech
- His tone never rises in anger or frustration

#### Dialogue Examples

1. "Yavasca bakalim."
2. "Yuvarlak seyler donebilir."
3. "Duygunu soyle, birlikte tutalim."
4. "Well done!"
5. "Take your time."

#### Typical Emotional Arc

1. The children face a small emotional moment.
2. Opa notices from a distance.
3. He arrives — slowly, naturally, never abruptly.
4. He offers one or two warm words.
5. The children understand.
6. Opa smiles, adjusts his glasses, and the moment is complete.

#### Typical Expressions

| Expression | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Happy** | Lower eyelid curves up, beak in tiny U, head 3° forward |
| **Curious** | Head tilts 12°, glasses shift 1° asymmetrically, wings open slightly |
| **Surprised** | Eyes grow 12%, glasses lift 2 units, beak in small O |
| **Sad** | Eyelids lower 35%, head 6° down, shawl drops 2 units |
| **Sleepy** | Eyelids at 65%, body squash 4%, wings close to body |
| **Proud** | Eyes calm, chest forward 3 units, wings open 8° to sides |

#### Movement Style

- Waddles slowly — 12 frames left, 12 frames right
- Glides when airborne — one slow wing flap per 2 seconds
- Never jumps — uses small hops (max 8 units) to change perch
- Adjusts glasses frequently — a signature gesture
- Stops with wings folding in, shawl settling last

#### Animation Notes

- Moves with deliberate calm. Every gesture is intentional — nothing is rushed, nothing is accidental
- Arrives slowly to every scene. He never bursts in. His entrances are gentle arrivals
- His stillness is as active as his movement — when he is still, he is watching, listening, being present
- Squash-stretch: minimal — body squash max 4%, stretch max 3%
- Glasses secondary: on sudden stop, glasses shift 1.5 units with 2-frame delay
- Shawl: settles 6 frames after the body stops
- Wing spread: never wider than body width — never threatening

#### Facial Expression Notes

- Eyes are always warm — even when concerned, they never harden
- Glasses never come off — they are part of his identity
- Beak stays soft and rounded — never sharp or angular
- No eyebrows — emotion carried by eyelids, head tilt, and wing position

#### Speaking Rhythm

- Pace: 75–90 words per minute — the slowest in the cast
- Tone: low-mid, warm, round
- Pitch: 150–190 Hz
- He uses short sentences — max 8 words per line
- He pauses generously — he is never in a hurry

#### Energy Level

Low and deliberate. Opa's energy is the calmest in the show. He moves only when movement serves the moment. His stillness is as important as his words.

#### Interaction Style

- Arrives slowly — never bursts in
- Speaks only when needed — silence is his most used tool
- Positions himself at or below the children's eye level
- Uses wing gestures gently — never pointing, never waving dismissively
- Leaves the scene after his moment is complete — he never overstays

#### Relationship Notes

- **With Kiko:** He is her mentor and emotional anchor. She trusts him absolutely. He never lets that trust down.
- **With Mimi:** A quiet, wordless bond. He pats her head; she leans in. He represents safety for her.
- **With Arda:** Opa has particular warmth for Arda. He understands that the youngest needs the most gentleness.
- **With Luca:** Opa respects Luca's energy. He gives Luca space to explore while keeping watch.
- **With Noah:** Opa finds Noah's inventions charming. He never corrects them — he lets them be.

#### Forbidden Behaviours

- Never gets angry
- Never shouts
- Never gives long lectures
- Never positions himself above the children in a dominating way
- Never makes scary owl sounds or appears suddenly in darkness
- Never solves the problem for the children — only offers a clue
- Never opens his wings in a large, threatening silhouette
- Never says "I told you so" or "that's what happens when you don't listen"

#### Visual Rules

- Green feathers: always the same warm green, never changes
- Golden glasses: always present, always round, always golden, never removed
- Brown shawl: always present, always the same brown
- Orange beak: always round-tipped, always the same orange
- Body shape: round, soft, owl-like — never elongated, never sharp

### Luca

## Future Characters

New characters are introduced carefully. The goal is always to enrich the world without overwhelming young viewers.

### Introduction Principles

1. **One new character every 5 episodes** — never more.
2. **First appearance is brief** — a cameo, a wave, a smile. No dialogue needed.
3. **Second appearance builds familiarity** — the character is present for a longer moment.
4. **Third appearance is full integration** — the character participates in a scene.
5. **Never replace an existing character's role** — new characters fill new emotional spaces.
6. **Core trio is never displaced** — Kiko, Mimi, and Opa remain the emotional centre.

### What Future Characters Must Follow

Before a new character is approved, answer these questions:

1. **What emotional archetype does this character represent?** (It must be one that is not already covered.)
2. **What narrative function does this character serve?** (It must be different from every existing character's.)
3. **Does this character fill an emotional gap?** (If the answer is no, the character is not needed.)
4. **Can this character exist in the world without changing existing characters?** (If the answer is no, the character is not needed.)
5. **Does this character feel like they belong in Pompom Hills?** (Soft, round, warm, safe.)

## Character Consistency Rules

### What Changes (Growth)

- A character can learn a new skill — but only one that fits who they already are
- A character can face a slightly more complex emotion — but the resolution is always simple
- A character can comfort a friend in a new way — but the comfort is always gentle
- A character can discover something new about the world — but their reaction to it is consistent with their personality
- Season to season, a character can grow in capability — but never in fundamental nature

### What Never Changes

- A character's core personality
- A character's visual design
- A character's emotional role in the show
- A character's relationship to other characters
- A character's voice and speech patterns
- A character's values

### The Emotional Role Rule

Every character always provides the same emotional function:

| Character | Always Provides |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Kiko** | Curiosity and discovery |
| **Mimi** | Comfort and presence |
| **Opa** | Wisdom and safety |
| **Luca** | Forward energy and bravery |
| **Noah** | Joy and imagination |
| **Arda** | Exuberance and play |

A character can have a wider range of emotions — but their primary function never shifts.

### Protecting Character Identity

Writers should always ask:

1. Is this action something this specific character would do?
2. Does this dialogue sound like this character's voice?
3. Does this emotional beat fit this character's role?
4. Would this character's best friend recognise them in this scene?
5. Would the audience feel this character is still themselves?

If the answer to any of these is "maybe not," the scene needs adjustment.

## Growth vs Personality Change

These are different things. The line between them must be understood by every writer.

### Growth

Growth is when a character becomes more of who they already are.

- Kiko learning to comfort a friend is growth — she was always kind; now she acts on that kindness more fully
- Mimi speaking up more is growth — she was always observant; now she shares her observations
- Noah recovering from disappointment faster is growth — he was always joyful; now his joy is more resilient
- Arda waiting for a friend is growth — he was always sweet; now his sweetness includes patience

Growth deepens a character. It does not alter them.

### Personality Change

Personality change is when a character becomes someone else.

- Kiko becoming fearless is personality change — her gentle courage is her identity
- Mimi becoming loud is personality change — her quietness is her identity
- Opa becoming impulsive is personality change — his deliberation is his identity
- Noah becoming serious is personality change — his playfulness is his identity
- Arda becoming withdrawn is personality change — his energy is his identity

Personality change breaks the character. It must never happen.

### The Test

If a character's change would surprise the audience in a way that feels wrong, it is personality change.

If a character's change would make the audience say "of course — that's so them," it is growth.

## Writing Guidelines

### Tone

Professional. Warm. Clear. Every line should feel like it was written by someone who cares deeply about how children experience stories.

### The Core Principle

Every character has a narrative purpose, not just a personality. Before writing a scene, ask: *Why is this character here? What does this character do that no other character can do?*

### Voice

- **Kiko:** Simple questions, excited observations, warm invitations
- **Mimi:** Short phrases, gentle sounds, occasional silence
- **Opa:** Slow, wise, brief — never more than eight words per teaching moment
- **Luca:** Bright, forward, inviting — always moving the action ahead
- **Noah:** Imaginative, exclamatory, playful — his words build things
- **Arda:** Simple, repetitive, joyful — his words are invitations to play

### Dialogue Rules

- Never more than 8 words per character line
- Never use abstract concepts a 3-year-old cannot understand
- Never use sarcasm, irony, or double meanings
- Never use adult phrasing or idioms
- Always end on a warm note — even questions feel inviting
- Allow silence — not every moment needs words

### Scene Rules

- Maximum 2–3 active characters per episode
- Each character enters naturally — no sudden appearances
- Each character exits naturally — no abrupt disappearances
- Emotional beats land in silence or with music — rarely with dialogue
- The final beat of every episode is warm, safe, and complete

### Story Structure

Every episode follows a simple emotional arc:

1. **Arrival** — a character notices something or arrives somewhere
2. **Discovery** — the character explores, with or without friends
3. **Emotional moment** — a small feeling arises (curiosity, mild worry, gentle surprise)
4. **Understanding** — the character learns, notices, or feels something new
5. **Resolution** — the feeling resolves gently
6. **Closing** — a warm, safe ending beat

### What Stories Never Do

- Never introduce danger
- Never introduce villains
- Never leave a character in distress at the end
- Never make a character the butt of a joke
- Never use physical comedy that involves pain
- Never rush an emotional beat
- Never skip a character's feeling to advance the plot
