# Pompom Hills — Series Structure & Character Strategy

---

## 1. Main Character Strategy

### The Question

Should Pompom Hills have one main character, two main characters, an ensemble cast, or a hybrid model?

### Analysis

| Model | Pros | Cons | Suitability (3-4y) |
|-------|------|------|----------------------|
| **One main character** | Maximum clarity, easiest emotional attachment, simplest storytelling | Limits episode variety, can feel repetitive | High |
| **Two main characters** | Good balance, natural pairing, shared load | Toddlers may split attention, unclear "anchor" | Medium |
| **Ensemble cast** | Rich variety, many stories possible | Confusing for very young viewers, dilutes attachment, hard to track | Low |
| **Hybrid: one primary + core cast** | Best of both — clear anchor with variety and growth potential | Requires discipline in screen time allocation | High |

### Recommendation

**Hybrid model: One primary main character + two core supporting main characters + occasional character-led episodes.**

**Why this is the right choice:**

1. **Toddlers aged 3 to 4 years need a single emotional anchor.** At this age, children attach to one character who represents them on screen. That character must appear in almost every episode, lead most stories, and be instantly recognisable. Splitting this role between two characters risks weakening the attachment.

2. **The animal friend provides essential counterbalance.** A strong secondary character adds variety, gentle humour, and emotional range without competing for the "main" slot. Toddlers can love the animal friend without being confused about who leads the story.

3. **The guide character provides emotional safety.** An older, calm presence reassures toddlers that someone wise is always nearby. This character does not need to lead episodes — their power is in appearing at the right moments.

4. **Occasional character-led episodes prevent staleness.** After the audience knows the core trio well (15–20 episodes), allowing the animal friend or guide to lead an occasional episode adds freshness without breaking the structure.

5. **AI video production demands consistency.** Fewer primary characters means fewer designs to maintain, fewer prompt variations to manage, and fewer visual inconsistencies across generated episodes.

## 2. Recommended Model

### One Primary Main Character + Two Core Supporting Main Characters + Occasional Character-Led Episodes

### How This Works

**Primary Main Character (the child)**
- Leads 70–80% of all episodes
- Appears in every episode
- Is the audience's emotional proxy
- Drives the story through curiosity and discovery
- Asks the questions toddlers would ask
- Represents the toddler's emotional world

**Secondary Main Character (the animal friend)**
- Appears in almost every episode
- Supports the primary character's story
- Reacts, explores, plays, and provides gentle humour
- Leads 15–20% of episodes (starting from episode 15+)
- Represents loyalty, comfort, and quiet emotion

**Gentle Guide Character (the elder)**
- Appears in 50–70% of episodes (not every episode)
- Provides emotional reassurance and closure
- Offers soft guidance, never control
- Leads 5–10% of episodes (rare, special)
- Represents safety, wisdom, and unconditional warmth

**Later Side Characters**
- Introduced visually first (background, waving, passing by)
- Given speaking roles only after the core trio is firmly established
- Appear in Season 1 but do not lead episodes until Season 2
- Maximum one new character introduced per 5-episode block

### Why This Structure Works

The structure mirrors how toddlers actually form relationships: one primary attachment, a few trusted companions, and a safe adult presence. It is the emotional architecture of a toddler's real world, translated into a show format.

## 3. Why Not Full Ensemble From Episode 1?

### The Risks of Ensemble-First

**Attention Span**
Toddlers aged 3 to 4 years have an attention span of 2–4 minutes per focused activity. Introducing 4–5 distinct characters with their own personalities, colours, and names within this window is cognitively overwhelming. The child viewer cannot process who is who, who matters most, and who to emotionally track.

**Emotional Attachment**
Attachment takes time. A toddler needs to see the same character repeatedly — same face, same colour, same voice, same catchphrase — before forming a bond. Introducing multiple "main" characters simultaneously fragments this bonding process. The child may like all characters equally but feel deeply attached to none.

**Repetition**
Repetition is the engine of toddler learning. They need to see the same character, hear the same phrase, and follow the same structure many times before it becomes familiar and comforting. An ensemble cast dilutes repetition because each character gets fewer repetitions per episode.

**Recognition**
At this age, visual recognition is primary. Children identify characters by colour, shape, and silhouette. When multiple characters share "main" status, the child cannot quickly determine whose story this is. A single primary character eliminates this confusion.

**AI Video Consistency**
From a production standpoint, AI-generated video struggles with consistency when multiple characters share equal prominence. Each character requires specific prompts, exact colour codes, and precise descriptions. Fewer primary characters means fewer variables, fewer errors, and more consistent output.

**Simple Storytelling**
Toddler stories work best with a single point of view. One character wants something, tries something, feels something, and learns something. Multiple simultaneous storylines or shared leads create narrative complexity that toddlers cannot follow.

**Brand Clarity**
A show with one clear main character is easier to brand, easier to merchandise, and easier to market. The primary character becomes the face of the show. This is a commercial reality as much as a creative one.

### Conclusion

Full ensemble casting is appropriate for children aged 5+. For 3 to 4 years, a clear hierarchy is essential. The audience needs to know: this is my character, this is her friend, this is the helper. Everything else comes later.

## 4. Character-Led Episode Strategy

### Phase 1: Episodes 1–10 (Establishment)

| Character | Screen Time | Episode Leads | Purpose |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| Primary Main Character | 80% | 9 episodes | Establish as emotional anchor |
| Animal Friend | 15% | 1 episode | Establish as loyal companion |
| Guide Character | 5% | 0 episodes | Establish as safe presence |

**Goal**: By episode 10, the audience should feel deeply attached to the primary character and comfortably familiar with the animal friend and guide.

### Phase 2: Episodes 11–20 (Deepening)

| Character | Screen Time | Episode Leads | Purpose |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| Primary Main Character | 70% | 7 episodes | Maintain anchor, allow breathing room |
| Animal Friend | 20% | 2 episodes | Deepen emotional range, show independent stories |
| Guide Character | 10% | 1 episode | Show gentle leadership, model calm |

**Goal**: By episode 20, the audience should love all three characters but still instinctively see the primary character as "their" character.

### Phase 3: Episodes 21–26 (Expansion)

| Character | Screen Time | Episode Leads | Purpose |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| Primary Main Character | 60% | 4 episodes | Maintain dominance, share spotlight |
| Animal Friend | 25% | 1 episode | Fully established as co-adventurer |
| Guide / Side Characters | 15% | 1 episode | Begin expanding world |

**Goal**: By episode 26, the world can sustain occasional side character episodes without losing the core identity.

### Recommended Season 1 Ratio

For 26 episodes:

- **20 episodes** led by primary main character (77%)
- **4 episodes** led by animal friend (15%)
- **2 episodes** led by guide character or focused on world (8%)

This ratio preserves the anchor while allowing sufficient variety for a full season.

## 5. Season 1 Character Rules

### Maximum Speaking Characters Per Episode

- **Episodes 1–10**: Maximum 2 speaking characters per episode (primary + one other)
- **Episodes 11–20**: Maximum 3 speaking characters per episode (full trio)
- **No episode in Season 1** should have more than 3 speaking characters

### When the Guide Character Appears

- **Episodes 1–5**: Appears in 2–3 episodes total, brief appearances only
- **Episodes 6–10**: Appears in 4–5 episodes, slightly longer but still brief
- **Episodes 11–20**: Appears regularly, sometimes leads an episode
- **Rule**: The guide never appears in the first 3 episodes. The audience must bond with the primary character and animal friend first.

### Should the Primary Main Character Appear in Every Episode?

**Yes.** The primary main character appears in every single episode of Season 1. This is non-negotiable for this age group. The emotional anchor must be constant.

### When the Animal Friend Can Lead an Episode

- **Not before episode 10.** The audience needs 10 episodes of bonding with the primary character before the animal friend takes the lead.
- **Maximum 2 lead episodes in Season 1.** More than this risks diluting the primary character's dominance.
- **The animal friend's lead episode should still include the primary character**, even if in a supporting role. Complete absence of the primary character should be avoided in Season 1.

### When Side Characters Can Be Introduced

- **Episode 5 or later**: A side character can appear visually (background, waving)
- **Episode 10 or later**: A side character can speak a line or two
- **Episode 15 or later**: A side character can have a small role in a story
- **Season 2**: Side characters can lead their own episodes
- **Rule**: Never introduce more than one new character per 5-episode block

### How to Avoid Overwhelming Toddlers

1. **One new element per episode** — new colour, new object, new word, OR new character. Never two at once.
2. **Repeat, repeat, repeat** — the same character, same phrase, same structure, every episode.
3. **Limit visual complexity** — maximum 3 characters on screen, simple backgrounds, clean frames.
4. **Limit verbal complexity** — maximum 15–20 unique words per episode, repeated 3–4 times each.
5. **Consistent structure** — same opening, same closing, same emotional arc every time.

## 7. Main Character Role

### Personality

- **Curious**: Asks "What's that?" about everything. Notices small details others miss.
- **Gentle**: Never rough, always soft with people, animals, and nature.
- **Kind**: Helps friends naturally. Shares without being asked.
- **Brave**: Tries new things, sometimes with a moment of hesitation. The hesitation makes her relatable.
- **Openly emotional**: Shows happiness, sleepiness, confusion, and gentle sadness. Never suppresses feelings.

### Emotional Function

The main character is the **toddler's on-screen self**. She models:
- How to ask questions
- How to try new things
- How to feel nervous and then feel okay
- How to share and care
- How to say goodnight

She is not perfect. She gets confused, impatient, and shy. This is intentional — toddlers need to see their own struggles reflected.

### How She Asks Questions

- Short, simple: "What's that?" / "Why?" / "How?"
- Voice is soft and curious, never demanding
- She points at things with her whole hand
- She tilts her head when thinking
- She looks at the animal friend, then at the guide

### How She Makes Discoveries

- She notices something (a flower, a sound, a colour)
- She approaches slowly
- She touches or observes gently
- She reacts with wonder ("So pretty!")
- She shares the discovery with her friend

### How She Reacts to Small Problems

- She pauses (brief hesitation)
- She looks at her friend
- She tries something simple
- If it does not work, she asks the guide
- She tries again with support
- She succeeds and smiles

### How Toddlers Relate to Her

- She looks like them (small, round, soft)
- She feels like them (curious, sometimes nervous)
- She does what they do (asks questions, explores, rests)
- She is not an adult, not a superhero, not a fantasy — she is a toddler

### What Kind of Episodes She Should Lead

- Discovery episodes (new things in the world)
- Routine episodes (bedtime, eating, washing)
- Emotion episodes (feeling sleepy, feeling happy, feeling unsure)
- Social episodes (sharing, waiting, helping)
- Seasonal episodes (rain, snow, wind, flowers)

## 8. Animal Friend Role

### Personality

- **Quiet**: Speaks softly, observes more than he talks. Communicates through expression and movement.
- **Loyal**: Always nearby. Always following. Always present. Never abandons the main character.
- **Gentle**: Never rough. Soft with everything — grass, flowers, friends.
- **Playful**: Hops, bounces, and wiggles. Provides physical comedy through gentle movement.
- **Slightly cautious**: More careful than the main character. Creates natural dynamic: one rushes, one waits.

### Emotional Function

The animal friend represents **comfort and loyalty**. He is the character toddlers want to cuddle. He models:
- Being a good friend by being present
- Feeling calm when others are excited
- Expressing emotions through body language (ears up = happy, ears down = sad)
- Rest is okay

He is the emotional comfort object of the show.

### How He Creates Gentle Humour

- He hops when others walk
- His ears flop in funny ways
- He falls asleep at unexpected moments
- He tries to copy the main character and does it slightly differently
- He hides behind the main character when shy

### How He Supports the Main Child

- He follows her everywhere
- He copies her actions
- He sits next to her when she is uncertain
- He nuzzles against her when she is sad
- He hops ahead to show her the way

### When He Can Lead His Own Episode

- When the story is about a feeling he has (sleepiness, excitement, missing someone)
- When the story is about something he discovers independently
- When the story is about his special skill (hopping, observing, resting)
- **Only after episode 10**, and maximum 2 times in Season 1

### What Toddler Emotions He Represents

- Needing comfort
- Feeling sleepy
- Being shy
- Wanting to play but feeling tired
- Missing a friend
- Feeling safe with someone nearby

## 9. Guide Character Role

### Personality

- **Wise**: Knows many things. Shares knowledge gently, never lectures.
- **Calm**: Never rushes. Never panics. Never raises voice.
- **Patient**: Waits as long as needed. Repeats as often as needed.
- **Kind**: Never scolds. Never criticises. Never punishes. Only encourages.
- **Storyteller**: Teaches through soft stories, not instructions.

### Emotional Function

The guide character represents **unconditional safety**. She is the grandparent figure — the warm adult who is always there, always calm, never angry. She models:
- Listening without interrupting
- Helping without taking over
- Waiting without frustration
- Celebrating small victories

She is the emotional safety net. When something feels uncertain, she appears and everything feels okay.

### How She Supports Without Scolding

- She notices when someone is struggling
- She approaches gently
- She asks a soft question: "What's happening?"
- She offers a suggestion, not a command: "What if we try...?"
- She stays until the problem is resolved
- She celebrates: "Well done!"

### How She Helps Resolve Small Problems

- She does not fix the problem herself
- She guides the characters to find the solution
- She provides a tool (an umbrella, a blanket, a story)
- She provides reassurance: "It's okay. Take your time."
- She closes the emotional loop: "You did it! How wonderful!"

### How Often She Should Appear

- **Episodes 1–5**: 2–3 appearances, very brief (10–15 seconds)
- **Episodes 6–10**: 4–5 appearances, slightly longer
- **Episodes 11–20**: Regular appearances, sometimes leads
- **Rule**: She should never feel like she is always there. Her appearances should feel like gentle surprises — the child is glad to see her but does not expect her.

### What Kind of Episodes She Can Lead

- Routine episodes (story time, goodnight, listening, breathing)
- Wisdom episodes (sharing, patience, kindness)
- World episodes (explaining seasons, weather, nature)
- Comfort episodes (when someone is sad, scared, or unsure)

### What She Must Never Be

- A strict teacher
- A disciplinarian
- A know-it-all
- A controlling adult
- A character who takes over the story
- A character who makes children feel wrong

She is always warm. Always. Without exception.

### Introduction Rules

1. **No more than one new side character at a time.** Introducing two or more new characters in the same episode overwhelms toddlers.
2. **Side characters should appear visually before becoming story leads.** Wave from background in episode X. Speak a line in episode X+5. Have a small role in episode X+10. Lead an episode in Season 2.
3. **Side characters should have simple, clear roles.** Each side character represents one thing: music, gardening, cooking, sleeping, building. One trait, one function, one personality.
4. **No villains.** The world of Pompom Hills has no antagonists. Conflicts come from situations (lost toy, rain, sleepiness), never from other characters.
5. **No mean characters.** No character is ever unkind. No character teases, mocks, or excludes. All characters are gentle.
6. **No danger-based conflict.** No character is ever in real danger. No character is ever hurt. No character is ever truly lost.

## 11. Story Rules

### What Every Episode Must Have

1. **One small situation** — rain, a lost toy, bedtime, a flower, a colour, a sound
2. **One emotional or developmental learning point** — sharing, waiting, trying, resting, feeling
3. **One repeated phrase** — said 3–4 times by different characters
4. **Very simple English** — maximum 15–20 unique words
5. **Slow pacing** — nothing rushed, nothing sudden
6. **No fast editing** — gentle cuts, long holds, smooth transitions
7. **No villains** — no character is the "bad guy"
8. **No danger** — no character is ever truly at risk
9. **No punishment** — no character is ever scolded, sent away, or made to feel wrong
10. **No shouting** — all dialogue is soft, gentle, warm
11. **No sarcasm** — all communication is sincere
12. **No intense sadness** — feelings are acknowledged gently, never dwelled upon
13. **No scary darkness** — night is soft blue, never black; the moon is friendly
14. **No complex plot** — one situation, one feeling, one resolution

### What Every Episode Should Feel Like

A warm hug. A soft voice. A gentle discovery. A safe ending. The toddler should feel held, not challenged. Comforted, not stimulated. Familiar, not surprising.

## 12. Standard Episode Formula

### Base Formula (7 minutes)

| Section | Duration | Description |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Opening Routine** | 10–20 seconds | Familiar music, same visuals, same greeting. Creates ritual and safety. |
| **Simple Discovery / Problem** | 30–60 seconds | One small thing happens. A sound, a sight, a feeling. |
| **Gentle Exploration** | 60–120 seconds | Characters investigate, try, react, learn. |
| **Soft Resolution** | 30 seconds | Problem solved gently. Everyone happy. Emotional loop closed. |
| **Repetition / Song / Goodbye** | 20–40 seconds | Key phrase repeated. Mini melody. Warm goodbye. |

### Variation by Episode Type

**Main Character Discovery Episodes**
- Opening routine → Kiko sees/hears something → asks "What's that?" → explores with Mimi → feels briefly unsure → Opa appears → problem resolved → "Well done!" → goodbye
- Focus: curiosity, discovery, emotional growth

**Animal Friend Feeling Episodes**
- Opening routine → Mimi has a feeling (sleepy, excited, sad) → Kiko notices → tries to help → Mimi's feeling is acknowledged → resolution through comfort or routine → goodbye
- Focus: emotional recognition, empathy, comfort

**Guide Character Calm Episodes**
- Opening routine → Opa invites Kiko and Mimi to a routine (story, goodnight, listening) → they participate → gentle lesson woven in → "Well done!" → goodbye
- Focus: routine, patience, wisdom

**World Discovery Episodes**
- Opening routine → Kiko discovers something in the world (pompom tree, moon, flowers) → explores with Mimi → Opa explains softly → wonder moment → goodbye
- Focus: nature, seasons, the world itself

**Bedtime Episodes**
- Opening routine → evening setting → Kiko is tired but not ready to sleep → gentle activity (story, song, counting) → sleepiness deepens → Opa says goodnight → Kiko and Mimi fall asleep → stars appear → goodbye
- Focus: bedtime routine, comfort, safety

## 14. Character Balance Recommendation

### Final Recommendations

**Should the show have one main character?**
Yes. Kiko is the emotional anchor. She appears in every episode, leads most stories, and is the character toddlers will attach to. This is not debatable for the target age group.

**Should the animal friend have their own stories?**
Yes, but sparingly. Mimi can lead 2–4 episodes per season, starting no earlier than episode 10. His stories should focus on feelings (sleepiness, shyness, excitement) rather than discovery. He should always be supported by Kiko's presence, even if she is not the lead.

**Should the guide character have their own stories?**
Rarely. Opa can lead 1–2 episodes per season maximum. Her episodes should focus on routine, story, and wisdom. She should never dominate the show. Her power is in her occasional, gentle presence.

**How often should each character lead?**
- Kiko: 77% of episodes (20 of 26 in Season 1)
- Mimi: 15% of episodes (4 of 26 in Season 1)
- Opa: 4% of episodes (1 of 26 in Season 1)
- World/Shared: 4% of episodes (1 of 26 in Season 1)

**Should side characters be introduced in Season 1 or later?**
Visually: yes, starting from episode 8–10. Speaking roles: maximum one side character speaks in Season 1. Leading episodes: no — save for Season 2. This keeps Season 1 simple, focused, and emotionally coherent.

## 15. Revised Series Pitch

**Pompom Hills** is a gentle animated series for toddlers aged 3 to 4 years, built around one clear emotional anchor: Kiko, a curious little girl who discovers the world one small wonder at a time. She is joined by Mimi, her loyal rabbit best friend who hops alongside her through every adventure, and Opa, a wise old owl who watches over them with warmth, patience, and soft-spoken wisdom. Together, they live in a cosy valley of rounded hills, pompom trees, spiral paths, and pastel houses — a world that feels like a soft toy come to life. Each episode follows one small situation — hearing rain, sharing a snack, planting a flower, saying goodnight — and resolves it gently, with repetition, kindness, and a soft "boop" of magic. The show speaks very simple English, moves at a toddler's pace, and never introduces danger, darkness, or conflict. Kiko leads most episodes, with occasional stories focused on Mimi's feelings or Opa's quiet wisdom. As the series grows, new characters may appear gently in the background, but the core trio always remains at the heart. Pompom Hills is designed to be visually consistent across AI-generated episodes, emotionally safe for the youngest viewers, and warm enough to become a toddler's favourite place to visit again and again.
