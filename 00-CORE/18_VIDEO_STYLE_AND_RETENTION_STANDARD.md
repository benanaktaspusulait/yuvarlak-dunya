# 18 — Video Style and Retention Standard

> This is a style and audience-retention standard, based on external feedback translated into
> production-safe language.
> It does NOT redesign characters. It does NOT redesign worlds.
> It does NOT change current episode scripts unless specifically requested.
> It does NOT add aggressive YouTube/Meta tactics, loud music, meme editing, fast cuts, or clickbait.
> It does NOT add on-screen text inside master videos by default.
>
> Cross-referenced from `00-CORE/17_VIDEO_GENERATION_STANDARD.md` and
> `00-CORE/30_YOUTUBE_METADATA_STANDARD.md`.

---

## 1. Purpose of This Standard

This document defines the visual, emotional, pacing and retention style for Pompom Hills videos.

Pompom Hills should feel:

- warm
- safe
- gentle
- colourful
- emotionally clear
- preschool-friendly
- visually premium
- calm but alive
- story-driven
- not noisy
- not over-edited
- not clickbait

The goal is not to make videos faster or louder.

The goal is to make every second feel alive, clear and emotionally meaningful for preschool children.

---

## 2. Core Style Strengths to Preserve

Preserve the current strengths of Pompom Hills:

- high-quality preschool animation look
- soft handcrafted visual style
- rounded character designs
- memorable characters such as Kiko, Mimi, Luca, Noah and Opa
- warm, colourful, safe environments
- soft textures
- gentle light
- clear emotional expressions
- calm sound design
- parent-friendly values
- stories about sharing, helping, curiosity, kindness, friendship and discovery

Do not sacrifice these strengths for short-term retention tricks.

Pompom Hills should look and feel premium, safe and trustworthy.

---

## 3. Visual Style Rule

Pompom Hills visuals must remain:

- pastel
- warm
- low contrast
- soft shadowed
- rounded
- readable
- child-safe
- handcrafted
- non-photorealistic

Avoid:

- harsh contrast
- realistic textures
- cinematic grading
- dramatic lighting
- cluttered backgrounds
- neon colours
- scary shadows
- overly busy action
- fast camera moves

Every frame should feel like it belongs to a gentle preschool animation universe.

---

## 4. First 3–5 Second Hook

Every video must create curiosity within the first 3–5 seconds.

Pompom Hills should prefer an **in-story hook**, not a disconnected teaser.

Good hooks:

- a character notices something
- a small surprise happens
- a question is asked
- a character reacts emotionally
- a prop appears
- a gentle problem begins
- a world element moves
- a character points, pauses, listens or wonders

Avoid slow empty openings where characters only stand and look without change.

Do not use loud jump cuts or aggressive teaser editing.

---

## 5. Flash-Forward Teasers

External feedback suggested placing a high-energy later moment at the beginning ("put the exciting part first").

**This is not adopted as the default.** A flash-forward teaser can attract attention, but for preschool viewers it risks breaking story continuity and confusing the narrative — the child has not yet met the moment being previewed.

**Default approach:** Use a natural, in-story hook inside the opening scene (§4). Do not open with a later moment out of context.

**Optional approach:** A 1–2 second flash-forward teaser may be used only when all of the following hold:

- the story remains clear
- the teaser does not confuse preschool viewers
- the teaser does not break world continuity
- the teaser is gentle, not loud
- the teaser is visually understandable
- the episode still begins naturally after it

For standard Pompom Hills episodes, avoid flash-forward teasers unless specifically approved per episode.

---

## 6. Pacing Standard

Pompom Hills pacing should be calm but never empty.

Every 3–5 seconds, something small should change.

Examples:

- a character looks up
- a character points
- a character smiles
- a prop moves
- a cloud drifts
- grass moves
- a ball rolls
- a character reacts
- a character takes one step
- a character listens
- a character shares
- a small problem appears
- a small solution begins

Avoid repeating the same visual idea for too long.

A 15-second shot should normally contain 3–5 micro-beats.

Example 15-second shot structure:

```text
0–3s:   Visual hook or action begins
3–6s:   Character reaction
6–9s:   World or prop responds
9–12s:  Dialogue or emotional beat
12–15s: Resolution, hold, or transition setup
```

---

## 7. Camera Dynamics

External feedback suggested adding zoom-in during action moments.

**This is not adopted literally.** Sudden or fast zooms conflict with the existing Pompom Hills camera language (`00-CORE/17_VIDEO_GENERATION_STANDARD.md` §Camera Rules, `00-CORE/VISUAL_STYLE_GUIDE.md`), which already forbids fast zooms, shake and whip pans.

The feedback is translated as: **gentle camera settle / soft follow / slight framing emphasis** — never a sudden zoom.

Allowed:

- gentle camera settle toward the emotional subject
- slow child-eye-level follow
- soft framing shift
- subtle pan
- very slow push, only if emotionally justified
- stable wide or medium hold

Not allowed:

- fast zoom
- sudden zoom-in
- handheld shake
- whip pan
- dramatic crash zoom
- fast camera chase
- cinematic action movement

For chase or movement scenes:

- Allowed: gentle pan following the action, slow tracking movement, slight settle toward the characters
- Not allowed: fast zoom, sudden zoom-in, handheld shake, whip pan, dramatic crash zoom, fast camera chase, cinematic action movement

The camera should support the emotion, not call attention to itself. The camera language remains preschool-safe and consistent with `00-CORE/17_VIDEO_GENERATION_STANDARD.md`.

---

## 8. Action and Energy

Longer videos need more internal movement than 15-second intro videos.

For 60–90 second videos:

- include a clear story problem
- include a clear emotional progression
- use props actively
- let the world participate
- add small physical actions
- avoid repeated looking-only moments
- avoid over-explaining with dialogue

Good action examples:

- ball rolls away
- first raindrop lands
- cloud moves
- flower sways
- character shares a toy
- character gently searches
- character notices a colour
- character helps a friend

Action should be simple, safe and readable.

---

## 9. Ending Standard

External feedback suggested ending with on-screen educational text ("lesson learned" text card).

**This is not adopted.** It conflicts with the existing Text Safety rule (`00-CORE/17_VIDEO_GENERATION_STANDARD.md` §Text Safety, `00-CORE/PRE_RENDER_CHECKLIST.md`, `00-CORE/INTERIOR_EXTERIOR_GUIDE.md`): no on-screen text, no speech bubbles, no captions, no subtitles burned into the master render.

Pompom Hills master videos should **not** use on-screen lesson text by default.

Instead, reinforce the theme through:

- a final character line
- a shared smile
- a small gesture
- a prop action
- a warm visual hold
- a parent-friendly caption **outside** the video (description or social caption — see §16)

Good endings:

- "Together!"
- "Hello, rain!"
- "Your turn."
- "We found it!"
- a quiet smile
- characters looking at the world together
- holding the shared object together

Avoid:

- large text overlays
- "Lesson learned" cards
- classroom-style moral statements
- subtitles or speech bubbles burned into the master render
- aggressive calls to action inside the video

If platform-specific text is needed, add it only in social captions, descriptions, or optional Shorts overlays — never in the master episode render.

---

## 10. Educational Value Style

Pompom Hills teaches through story, not lectures.

Educational themes should be embedded in:

- character interaction
- world discovery
- simple choices
- repeated actions
- emotional cause and effect

Avoid teacher-like dialogue.

Prefer:

```text
Luca: "Can I try?"
Noah: "I want to try too."
Luca: "Your turn."
```

over:

```text
"We should learn that sharing is important."
```

The child should feel the lesson through the moment.

---

## 11. Sound and Voice Style

Preserve the current sound strengths:

- soft character voices
- warm tone
- gentle emotional delivery
- calm ambience
- non-overwhelming sound effects

Sound should never tire preschool viewers.

Avoid:

- loud music
- sudden stingers
- intense whooshes
- aggressive sound design
- noisy backgrounds
- scary thunder unless story-safe and very soft

Voice performance should remain consistent across shots. Character voices should not change pitch, age, timbre or emotional identity. See `00-CORE/AUDIO_GUIDE.md`.

---

## 12. Retention Without Clickbait

Pompom Hills should improve retention through clarity and warmth, not clickbait.

Good retention tools:

- clear first 3-second visual question
- simple prop-based story
- expressive faces
- micro-actions
- gentle surprise
- emotional payoff
- warm ending
- familiar characters
- familiar worlds

Bad retention tools:

- loud edits
- misleading teaser
- exaggerated drama
- fake urgency
- flashing text
- overactive camera
- chaotic movement

---

## 13. Long-Form Video Length Guidance

Current preferred long-form lengths:

- 60 seconds
- 90 seconds

120-second videos are allowed only when:

- 60–90 second videos show strong retention
- the story has enough beats
- the world can support longer attention
- the episode can produce multiple strong Shorts
- pacing remains alive every 3–5 seconds

Do not make a video longer just because longer videos are possible. Length must be earned by story beats.

(Consistent with the video length strategy in `11-DOCS/09_YOUTUBE_STRATEGY.md` — Channel Growth Decisions §6.)

---

## 14. Long Video Structure

A strong 60–90 second Pompom Hills episode should usually include:

1. Opening curiosity
2. Small desire or question
3. Gentle problem or discovery
4. Thinking or emotional pause
5. Action / play / interaction
6. Resolution
7. Warm final hold

Example: *My Turn, Your Turn*

- they see the ball
- both want it
- one shares
- they play together
- ball rolls away
- they find it together
- warm friendship ending

---

## 15. Shorts Extraction Rule

Long videos should generate Shorts. But Shorts are not random cuts.

Each Short must have:

- hook
- small action
- emotional beat
- payoff

Shorts may use more direct titles and hooks than long videos, but still remain gentle and preschool-safe.

No meme editing. No loud overlay. No misleading title.

(See `00-CORE/30_YOUTUBE_METADATA_STANDARD.md` §13 for Shorts metadata requirements.)

---

## 16. Platform Caption Questions

Instead of on-screen lesson text (§9), use soft caption questions in the description or social caption — never burned into the video.

Examples:

- Who would you share the ball with?
- What colour flower should Kiko find?
- Have you seen clouds like these?
- Which Pompom Hills friend should visit next?
- Can you say "your turn"?

Questions should be parent-friendly and simple.

Do not pressure children to comment. Do not use aggressive CTA language.

(Consistent with `00-CORE/30_YOUTUBE_METADATA_STANDARD.md` §14 CTA Rules and `11-DOCS/09_YOUTUBE_STRATEGY.md` §10 Engagement Style.)

---

## 17. Public Link Rule

Never use YouTube Studio links in public captions, descriptions, or social posts.

Do not use:

```text
https://studio.youtube.com/...
```

Use only public YouTube links.

Default channel link:

```text
https://www.youtube.com/@PompomHills
```

Public video links must be normal YouTube watch links, not Studio links.

---

## 18. Episode Style QA Checklist

Before approving a Pompom Hills long video, check:

```
✓ Does the first 3–5 seconds create curiosity?
✓ Is the opening hook part of the story (not a disconnected teaser)?
✓ Does something small change every 3–5 seconds?
✓ Are there any repeated empty beats?
✓ Is the camera calm but alive?
✓ Are there no sudden zooms or fast pans?
✓ Is the lesson shown through action, not lectured?
✓ Does the ending feel warm?
✓ Is there no unnecessary on-screen text?
✓ Are voices gentle and consistent?
✓ Is the world visually stable?
✓ Are colours stable and not drifting?
✓ Can the video produce at least 3 Shorts?
✓ Would a parent understand the value of the video?
✓ Would a preschool child understand the emotional moment?
```

Run alongside `00-CORE/MASTER_QA_CHECKLIST.md` before publish.

---

## 19. How to Apply External Feedback

External feedback about pacing, editing or retention should be translated into Pompom Hills production language before being applied. Do not adopt aggressive tactics literally.

| External feedback says | Translate to |
|---|---|
| "Make it faster" | Add more meaningful micro-actions (§6), not faster cuts. |
| "Add zoom" | Use gentle camera settle / soft follow / slight framing emphasis (§7), not a sudden zoom. |
| "Add text lesson" | Use a final character line, visual action, or platform caption question (§9, §16), not on-screen text. |
| "Put the exciting part first" | Strengthen the in-story opening hook (§4). Use a flash-forward only if it does not break preschool clarity or story continuity (§5), and only with specific approval. |

Any future external feedback should be run through this same translation step before being turned into a production instruction.

---

## 20. Final Principle

Pompom Hills should remain: premium-looking, gentle, safe, warm, clear, emotionally sincere, and preschool-readable.

Retention should improve because the stories are alive and emotionally clear — not because they are loud, fast, or over-edited.

Every production decision should protect the long-term identity of the Pompom Hills universe.
